Video Transitions

Transitions can be an important part of your video. They are used to organize visual flow, maintain clarity, and enhance understanding. Transitions add another layer of visual aesthetics and organization that can be extremely useful to your audience. They make your video feel smoother, more polished, and simulate an intuitive flow. More benefits of transitions include:

  • Capturing Attention: transitions can add visual interest to your video and allow viewers to stay oriented when presented with content

  • Improved Storytelling: transitions can help signal a shift in topic or environment in a video

  • Improved Visual Hierarchy: transitions can reduce the abruptness of new content appearing on the screen and designate the importance of the new information

  • Cultivating Brand Identity: unique, brand-specific transitions that are consistently used can cultivate a sense of identity for your company

If you include transitions that are intuitively placed and help provide structure, users will be more enticed to return to your video. However, there is a very fine balance between subtle emphasis and possibly overwhelming. Some users may react normally to transitions, but it is important to remember that other users may have sensitivities or difficulties you did not anticipate. Disabled users may be sensitive to aspects of transitions that are commonly looked over. Transitions can help your users use visual intuition on the flow of information, but using them in an ineffective or inordinate manner can turn users off your video permanently.

Disability and Transitions

When designing your transitions, it is important to consider visual appeal and organizational flow. It is also important to consider how these transitions affect disabled users who may not be able to see them or would have an adverse reaction to them. Some disabilities to consider include:

  • Vestibular disorders (vertigo)

  • Epilepsy

  • Users with cognitive/attention disorders

  • Visual impairments

Vestibular and epileptic disorders can be very harmful and easy to trigger. It is imperative that you keep these users in mind when designing quick and flashy transitions, and it is often better to stay away from them. Users who suffer from migraine headaches also could be triggered by unnecessary motion, and the effects could be severe.

Users with cognitive and attention disorders like ADHD or autism can be overstimulated and confused. Ensure your video uses purposeful, simple transitions that emphasize clarity, not confusion.

Users with visual impairments may be using captions or transcripts. A transition can interrupt the visibility and timing of closed captions, causing the user to incorrectly interpret or even miss information entirely.

WCAG provides guidelines regarding accessible transitions: when designing transitions, your goal should be to ensure users are not harmed or distracted by motion. Eliminate unnecessary motion. If you feel that the animation is essential to functionality, ensure that it abides by the provided guidelines to ensure user safety. Overall, it is important to remember that people can be harmed from your design choices.
