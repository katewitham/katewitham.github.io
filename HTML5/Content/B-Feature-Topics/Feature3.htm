Why does Accessibility Matter?

Professional and technical writers play a crucial role in shaping digital experiences for all different types of people. From creating documentation to marketing materials, to journalistic content, to so much more, writers influence how people from every part of the world access information. Accessibility matters because it expands your audience and aligns with both legal and ethical responsibilities. More importantly, it reflects a commitment to equity and inclusivity. When writers adopt inclusive practices, they help ensure that people with disabilities can participate in digital spaces, just as abled people can.

Crip Camp (2020)

The documentary Crip Camp offers a powerful illustration of why this commitment matters in practice. The film shows what an accessible world can look like when people with disabilities are not treated as afterthoughts but rather as full participants. At Camp Jened, disabled teenagers experienced, many for the first time, an environment built around their needs rather than one that forced them to adapt. The camp wasn’t “special” because it segregated them; it was transformative because it encouraged autonomy and treated accessibility as a baseline expectation. Seeing this environment in practice makes it clear that accessibility is needed. When barriers are removed, people thrive, connect, and contribute in ways that inaccessible spaces often prevent.

How does this translate into writing and web development?

For writers, this lesson translates directly into digital work. Just as the activists in Crip Camp fought for ramps, interpreters, and equal access to public buildings, today’s digital spaces require their own forms of access:

  • clear structure

  • readable text

  • captions

  • alt text

  • compatibility with assistive technologies.

These features aren’t add-ons. They are the digital equivalents of the ramps and curb cuts that activists demanded in the 1970s and 80s. When writers build accessibility into their content, they help create the kind of inclusive environment that Crip Camp shows is both possible and necessary. The more that professional and technical writers embrace accessibility, the closer we get to the kind of world Crip Camp envisions: one where everyone has the tools and opportunities to participate fully.

Web accessibility is not an optional add‑on. It is a fundamental part of responsible design. By understanding different types of disabilities and embracing inclusive design techniques, writers can create content that welcomes rather than excludes. As Crip Camp reminds us, accessibility is ultimately about dignity and equal participation. When professional and technical writers commit to accessibility, they contribute to a more inclusive digital world. One where everyone has the opportunity to engage, learn, and be heard.
